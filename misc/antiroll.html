Cryostat sectioning with anti-roll plate

Even though the best technique for good sections is the “brush technique” (dragging the sections towards you while rotating the handwheel), some people find anti-roll plate works better for them. Both techniques are useful depending on what you are sectioning and in what micron settings. However, getting the sections under the glass is challenging. The antiroll glass edge must be in precise position resting just a hair behind the edge of the disposable blade.

 

Before troubleshooting your antiroll glass sectioning make sure you have consistent advancing and good sections without using the antiroll plate. Otherwise, adjusting the position of the glass edge will be useless.

 

You can move the rectangular glass plate up and down and take sections until they go smoothly underneath the glass. If the sections do not go under the glass, rather they go upward, the glass is too behind the blade’s edge. If the sections are bunching in front of the glass, the specimen may be hitting the glass first and you must slightly retract the glass edge.

Check if the glass edge is chipped or has OCT deposits. You can smooth the glass edge by rubbing it with wool cloth.

 

Check if the blade is clean and without OCT deposits.

 

It is crucial that the speed of rotation of the handwheel, thus the speed of sections sliding under the glass, is the same. Try a faster speed if it does not compromise the quality of the sections.

 

A suitable temperature for the chamber (specimen) is also necessary. Colder specimens will shatter, and warmer specimens will smear. 

 

Check the positioning of the glass. If the glass edge is not parallel to the blade edge, you can easily adjust it by loosening the 3mm Allen screw on the antiroll swivel arm. Adjust the glass edge and tighten the screw.

 

Sections greater than 20 microns may be thicker than the gap between the glass and the blade. The section will jam in front of the glass plate. To increase the gap thickness, put tape at the left and right edges of the rectangular glass to lift it up a fraction.

 

Remember to watch where your fingers are in relation to the blade edge. You may want to remove the blade while performing adjustments.  

     

 Other things affecting the quality of sections are:

  • Brand of blade (some blades are slightly thinner than others) or get dull faster
  • Angle of the blade holder (it should be around 5 degrees)
  • Thickness of sections
  • Chamber temperature. Cold specimens will shatter, and warm specimens will smear
  • OCT medium quality and preparation of the specimen
  • Type of tissue (density, fat content)

 

Lastly, remember to stay calm and carefully observe your specimens, adjusting where needed.
